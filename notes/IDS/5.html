A Brief History of Networking - Slides

Claude Shannon and Information Theory

  • He did work on quantifying information.
    • We can think of information in terms of choices, coded with bits.
    • Encoding matters. The same bits can convey different messages
  • He worked for a phone company.
    • Wanted to know how much information could be successfully transmitted over a wire.
    • Showed that information transfer could be done reliably.

Early Military Computers

Military wanted missile defense:

  • Digitial Radar Relay - Experimented with sending radar data over phone lines.
  • Whirlwind at MIT - First interactive machine (not batch).
  • Put these together, made a machine that sensed a plane flying past and kept track in its memory.
  • SAGE - Semi-automatic Ground System, used to sense incoming air attacks.

Paul Baran and Packet Switching

  • Caused a shift in work from phone lines to digital lines.
  • Fault tolerance was exactly what the military wanted.
  • Led to ARPANet - connected a couple military and educational hubs.

Introduction to TCP/IP - Slides

High-Level of TCP/IP Stack

  • Link Layer
    • Sends bits from one machine to another.
    • Include ethernet, wi-fi, fiber optic, dial-up.
  • Network Layer
    • Packet delivery protocol.
    • Mostly IP.
  • Transport Layer
    • Make connection to another computer.
    • Send Datagrams from one place to another.
    • TCP and UDP.
  • Application Layer
    • Protocols that deliver to specific applications.
    • HTTP, FTP, SMTP.
  • User Layer
    • The actual programs a user is working with.

Internet Protocol (IP)

  • How we address/find any machine on the internet.
    • IPv4 is a 32-bit number that identifies any computer.
  • Best effort delivery.
  • Works with individual packets.
  • Must route uniformly, no matter the divisions between wire/radio etc. Abstracts boundaries.
  • Fragmentation: Allows us to send up to 65KB at a time, but hardware can break it up.
